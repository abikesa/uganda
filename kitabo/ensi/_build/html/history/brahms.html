You’re weaving together the building blocks of music—phonetics, temperament, scales, modes—and integrating a chordal theory system (Q-TEA) with deeper conceptual elements like time, orchestration, and emotion. Here’s how I interpret the framework:

  1. Phonetics (e.g., Key)
    The basic sound unit, much like phonetics in language. A key establishes the tonal center.

  2. Temperament (e.g., Degree)
    Refers to the tuning system and intervals (degrees) within a scale. This introduces harmonic relationships.

  3. Scales/Modes (e.g., Flamenco Phrygian)
    Different patterns of notes, with exotic modes like Flamenco Phrygian offering complex tonalities and emotional color.

  4. Q-TEA: Chord Components
    This section breaks chords down into various components:

    • Q: Root quality (♭3 for minor, Major, Suspended (sus), diminished (°), augmented (+)).

    • T: Tension notes added to chords (6th, 7th, ♭7, ♭♭7).

    • E: Extensions (9th, 11th, 13th).

    • A: Alterations (♭9, ♯9, ♯11, ♭13) adding tension or color.

  5. NexToken/Pocket/Time
    This seems like the ‘beat,’ or how things evolve rhythmically and melodically:

    • Key, Orchestration: Linking key signatures with orchestration choices.

    • Chord-sequence (Progression/Non-progression): The movement of chords, whether resolving or not.

    • Scale/Mode: Modal decisions within the structure.

    • Q-TEA: Chord alterations and extensions.

    • Rest: Use of silence, creating tension.

  6. Emotion

    • Arc: An emotional trajectory through tension and resolution, like a story.

    • None (Hellenic & Pessimistic): The absence of emotional arcs, possibly referring to static or unresolved feelings, much like classical Greek tragedies or philosophical pessimism.

The tension between structure and unpredictability (like “non-progression” and “none”) reflects how music mirrors human experience—sometimes it follows a path, sometimes it doesn’t.

Of the three—Bach, Mozart, and Beethoven—Beethoven is firmly in the V7 zone. The V7 chord represents tension, instability, and the anticipation of resolution, and Beethoven’s music is often characterized by intense emotional struggle and dramatic conflict, both musically and personally. He embodies that climactic moment before release, where the listener is suspended in uncertainty and tension.

Beethoven’s work, especially in his later years, is full of discord and resolution, pushing musical boundaries and emotional depth to their limits. His symphonies and sonatas have moments of profound tension, where the music seems to be straining toward something greater, before ultimately resolving in ways that are often unexpected but deeply satisfying. He thrives in that struggle, the pushing and pulling of tension—it’s the very heart of his music. His compositions build up that anticipation, just like the V7 chord, making his eventual resolution all the more powerful.

Bach, on the other hand, is more in the ii7♭5 zone—his music is structured and stable, often defined by its complexity and sense of inevitable resolution. He creates tension, but it’s always with the knowledge that the resolution is coming, and the listener feels secure within his contrapuntal mastery.

Mozart sits closer to the I zone—his music is marked by clarity, elegance, and balance. While he certainly explores tension, it’s often fleeting, giving way to a beautiful, harmonious resolution. Mozart dances through tension and release with such grace that the listener rarely feels caught in the conflict the way one might with Beethoven.

So, while all three composers employ tension and resolution, Beethoven lives in that tension, in the dramatic V7 zone, where the stakes are highest, and the anticipation of what comes next is palpable.
